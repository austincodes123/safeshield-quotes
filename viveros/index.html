Umbrella · The Next Layer

Every dollar you've built deserves a second fence around it.

An umbrella sits above your auto and home policies and takes over when those limits run out. For a household with a net worth comfortably north of $500,000, an umbrella is not optional — it's the lowest-cost, highest-impact dollar you spend on insurance.

Why this matters for you

One serious at-fault accident in a $250,000 vehicle, with a passenger injured, can burn through $500,000 in liability limits in an afternoon. Once those limits are exhausted, the injured party's attorney looks at your balance sheet — income, investments, real estate — to make up the difference.

A $1 million Markel umbrella sits on top of your new Progressive auto limits for $380 per year. Adding $1 million of Excess Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist — which protects you and Angela if you're hit by a driver with no insurance or insufficient coverage — brings the total to $522 per year. That's roughly $1.43 a day for seven figures of extra protection on both sides of the equation.

The net-worth test

The rule of thumb is simple: your umbrella limit should equal or exceed your net worth. If you're worth more than $500,000, an umbrella is no longer a nice-to-have — it's the piece of the puzzle that keeps a single bad day from undoing years of work.

One thing to flag on homes held in an LLC

A personal umbrella only extends over personally-owned assets and policies. If a home is titled in the name of an LLC, it's not covered by your personal umbrella and needs its own commercial liability layer. This doesn't affect your auto umbrella at all — but if there's LLC-owned real estate in the picture, we should review that separately to make sure there's no gap.
